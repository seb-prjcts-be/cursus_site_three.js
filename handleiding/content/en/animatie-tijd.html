Movement works in three.js the same way as in p5: change things a tiny bit every frame. Only you don't use frameCount but a Timer - and it thinks in seconds.

What you will learn
  • renderer.setAnimationLoop(fn) is your draw(): roughly 60 times per second
  • THREE.Timer → update() + getElapsed() gives the elapsed time in seconds
  • Time-based animation is framerate-independent: equally fast on every screen
  • Your p5 toolkit keeps working: Math.sin(), Math.cos() and lerp thinking

From frameCount to Timer

In p5 you often wrote rotateY(frameCount * 0.01). You can do that here too (mesh.rotation.y += 0.01), but there is a better habit: animate based on time.

const timer = new THREE.Timer();

renderer.setAnimationLoop(() => {
  timer.update();                     // internal update, once per frame
  const t = timer.getElapsed();       // seconds since the start

  mesh.rotation.y = t;                       // 1 radian per second
  mesh.position.y = Math.sin(t * 2) * 1.5;   // up and down

  renderer.render(scene, camera);
});

Why is this better? A phone at 30 fps and a gaming monitor at 144 fps now show exactly the same movement. With frame counting, your sketch runs almost five times as fast on the gaming monitor.

Rule of thumb for reading this: rotation.y = t means one radian per second, so a full turn takes about 6.3 seconds. Want it twice as fast? rotation.y = t * 2.

A wave of cubes

The classic p5 pattern "a row of things, each with a phase offset in sin()" works literally the same here. The only difference: the cubes already exist, you only change their y position.

Did you know

Look at the line with geom and mat: all fifteen cubes share one geometry and one material. three.js draws that much faster than fifteen separate copies - and it's exactly why geometry and material are kept separate.

Smoothly towards a target: lerp

The p5 pattern x = lerp(x, targetX, 0.1) exists built in on every position:

// Every frame 10% closer to the target - smooth easing
mesh.position.lerp(new THREE.Vector3(2, 1, 0), 0.1);

Positions in three.js are Vector3 objects, the 3D version of p5.Vector. You already know many of the methods: add(), sub(), normalize(), dist()... your vector knowledge from p5 simply moves along with you.

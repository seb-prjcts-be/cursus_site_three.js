The camera determines what you see and how it feels: a wide angle makes everything dramatic, a telephoto lens flattens everything out. And because the camera is an ordinary object, you can animate it like everything else.

What you will learn
  • The fov (field of view) is the viewing angle in degrees: small = telephoto, large = wide angle
  • camera.lookAt(x, y, z) points the camera at a point - essential after moving it
  • OrthographicCamera has no perspective: handy for isometric and graphic looks
  • The camera is an Object3D: position, animation and even groups just work on it

The four numbers of PerspectiveCamera

new THREE.PerspectiveCamera(
  75,                        // fov: viewing angle in degrees
  innerWidth / innerHeight,  // aspect: aspect ratio
  0.1,                       // near: anything closer is not drawn
  1000                       // far: anything further away is not drawn
);
  • fov - 45-60 is neutral, 75+ becomes an action game, 20 feels like binoculars. Comparable to perspective() in p5, but as a property.
  • near and far - the visible range. Objects outside that range disappear. Don't set far absurdly high "just to be safe": that costs precision and causes flickering surfaces.
Watch out

If you change fov, near or far after creating the camera, you have to call camera.updateProjectionMatrix() - the same rule as with window resize. Position and lookAt don't need that.

OrthographicCamera: without perspective

An orthographic camera draws parallel lines as genuinely parallel: far away is just as big as close by. That gives you the isometric look of city-building games and technical drawings - and it's the 3D equivalent of ortho() in p5:

const d = 5;   // how "wide" you look
const aspect = innerWidth / innerHeight;
const camera = new THREE.OrthographicCamera(
  -d * aspect, d * aspect,   // left, right
   d, -d,                    // top, bottom
   0.1, 100                  // near, far
);
camera.position.set(5, 5, 5);
camera.lookAt(0, 0, 0);
Did you know

With an orthographic camera, zooming in by moving the camera has no effect - everything stays the same size. You zoom by changing the viewing width (the d above) or camera.zoom, followed by updateProjectionMatrix().

Every three.js sketch starts with the same four-step ritual: scene, camera, renderer, loop. This is your new createCanvas reflex.

What you will learn
  • A Scene is the world all your objects live in
  • A PerspectiveCamera is the eye - in p5 it secretly existed already
  • The WebGLRenderer creates the canvas, like createCanvas()
  • setAnimationLoop() is your draw(): it runs about 60 times per second
  • Forget one of the four? Black screen.

The four steps

  1. Scene - the world

    In p5 the world exists implicitly: everything you draw ends up "somewhere". In three.js you create that world explicitly and add objects to it with scene.add(...).

  2. Camera - the eye

    The four numbers of PerspectiveCamera(75, aspect, 0.1, 1000): the field of view in degrees, the aspect ratio, and the minimum and maximum viewing distance. Move the camera back a bit (camera.position.z = 5), otherwise you're standing inside your object.

  3. Renderer - the canvas

    The renderer creates a real <canvas> element. Unlike p5, you have to attach it to the page yourself with document.body.appendChild(renderer.domElement).

  4. Loop - the draw()

    renderer.setAnimationLoop(fn) calls your function every frame. Inside it you change properties and finish with renderer.render(scene, camera): draw the world through the eye.

Try it yourself

Below is the full ritual, plus one spinning cube. Feel free to change things: alter the colour, the rotation speed, or camera.position.z - and click Run.

Try setting camera.position.z = 2 or = 20. You're moving the eye, not the cube. That distinction - camera versus object - is new compared to p5, where you usually shifted the world itself with translate().

What if it stays black?

The three classics, in order of likelihood:

  • Camera not moved - you're standing inside the object. Set camera.position.z = 5.
  • No light added while using MeshStandardMaterial - the object is there, but black on black.
  • Forgot renderer.render(scene, camera) in the loop - nothing ever gets drawn.
Did you know

setAnimationLoop has a hidden advantage over calling requestAnimationFrame yourself: it also works in VR (WebXR). That's why it's the recommended approach.

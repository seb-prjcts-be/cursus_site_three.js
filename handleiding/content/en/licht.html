In p5, lights() was a single command that switched "everything on". In three.js, lights are objects you place in the scene - and the combination of lights determines the entire mood of your image.

What you will learn
  • AmbientLight - light from everywhere, no shadow side (the "base lighting")
  • DirectionalLight - parallel rays like the sun, with a direction
  • PointLight - a light bulb: shines in all directions from a single point
  • The classic recipe: one DirectionalLight + one weak AmbientLight
  • Lights are objects: you can move them and even animate them

The standard recipe

This is the lighting you'll use in nine out of ten sketches:

// The "sun": strong, with a clear direction
const sun = new THREE.DirectionalLight(0xffffff, 3);
sun.position.set(5, 5, 5);
scene.add(sun);

// Soft fill so the shadow side isn't pitch black
scene.add(new THREE.AmbientLight(0xffffff, 0.3));

The two arguments are always colour and intensity. With DirectionalLight only the direction of the position matters: the light shines from there towards the origin, with parallel rays. How far away it sits makes no difference.

Which light when?

  • Outdoor scene, daylight - DirectionalLight + weak AmbientLight
  • Indoor scene, lamp or campfire - PointLight, possibly several
  • Spotlight on a stage - SpotLight: a cone with a direction and an angle
  • Soft studio look - HemisphereLight: sky colour from above, ground colour from below
Watch out

More lights = more computation per pixel. Two or three lights is normal; twenty PointLights will make your sketch slow. If you need lots of "points of light", use small MeshBasicMaterial spheres that imitate light - those cost almost nothing.

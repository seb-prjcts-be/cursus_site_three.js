Helpers: your eyes in 3D space

scene.add(new THREE.AxesHelper(3));      // red = x, green = y, blue = z
scene.add(new THREE.GridHelper(10, 10)); // 10x10 floor grid

// Where is my light actually pointing?
scene.add(new THREE.DirectionalLightHelper(sun, 1));

Remember the colour order with "xyz = rgb". Helpers cost almost nothing; feel free to leave them in until your sketch is finished, just like you put temporary debug text on the canvas in p5.

lil-gui: sliders in five lines

Stop the "change value, save, reload" cycle: lil-gui ships in your clone as an addon and builds a panel that lets you tune your scene live:

The workflow professionals use: build your scene, attach every "magic value" to a gui slider, tweak until it feels right, and write the values you found back into your code. Tweaking with sliders is ten times faster than reloading.

Staying fast: the three rules

  1. Create nothing in the loop

    No new THREE.something() inside setAnimationLoop. You create geometries, materials and vectors once; in the loop you only change values. Familiar from p5, but here it weighs heavier: creating a geometry is expensive.

  2. Share geometries and materials

    Fifty cubes with the same shape and colour = one geometry + one material + fifty meshes. Every unique combination of geometry and material is a separate "draw call" - the fewer, the faster.

  3. Thousands? InstancedMesh

    For serious numbers (a forest, confetti, a crowd) there is InstancedMesh: one object that draws thousands of copies in a single draw call, each with its own position and rotation. Look up the example webgl_instancing_dynamic in your clone when you need this.

Did you know

Want to see your framerate like frameRate() in p5? You do not type anything special in your browser console: open the Performance tab, or add the Stats addon (three/addons/libs/stats.module.js) for the classic fps counter in the top left.

Watch out

If you remove objects from a scene, their geometry and material stay in video memory until you call geometry.dispose() and material.dispose() yourself. For a sketch that simply keeps running it does not matter; for an app that switches scenes it does.

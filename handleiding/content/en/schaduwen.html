Shadows are the difference between shapes that float and a scene that feels real. They are off by default because they cost computing power - you switch them on in three places: the renderer, the light, and the objects.

What you will learn
  • Step 1: renderer.shadowMap.enabled = true - the main switch
  • Step 2: light.castShadow = true - only Directional-, Point- and SpotLight can do it
  • Step 3: per object castShadow (casts a shadow) and/or receiveShadow (catches shadows)
  • Blocky edges? Increase the resolution with light.shadow.mapSize

The three switches

three.js lets you choose per component, because every shadow costs performance. The complete recipe:

// 1. Renderer: main switch
renderer.shadowMap.enabled = true;
renderer.shadowMap.type = THREE.PCFSoftShadowMap;  // softer edges

// 2. Light: this light casts shadows
sun.castShadow = true;
sun.shadow.mapSize.set(2048, 2048);  // higher resolution = crisper edge

// 3. Objects: who casts, who catches
cube.castShadow = true;       // the cube casts a shadow
floor.receiveShadow = true;   // the floor catches them

An object can also do both: a sphere on a table casts a shadow onto the table and catches the shadow of a lamp above it.

Watch out

AmbientLight cannot cast shadows - by definition it comes from everywhere. If you see no shadow, go through the three switches in order; nine times out of ten exactly one of them is missing.

Try it: bouncing ball with a shadow

Watch how the shadow gets sharper as the ball approaches the floor and softer as it rises - PCFSoftShadowMap does that for you. Try disabling that line and compare.

If the shadow looks weird

  • Blocky edge - increase shadow.mapSize to 2048 or 4096 (costs memory).
  • Shadow cut off - with DirectionalLight the shadow area is a box around the origin. Large scene? Enlarge sun.shadow.camera.left/right/top/bottom.
  • Stripes on surfaces ("shadow acne") - play with sun.shadow.bias, for example -0.0005.
Did you know

Under the bonnet, three.js renders the scene an extra time from the light's point of view, into an invisible depth texture (the shadow map). That is why shadows cost performance: every shadow-casting light is an extra render per frame. p5 does not have this mechanism - this is typically the point where three.js shows its added value.

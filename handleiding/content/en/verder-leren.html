Your local treasure chamber

The manual you are in right now lives next to a complete copy of the three.js repository. That copy is not just the library - it is also documentation and inspiration:

  • Local examples - hundreds of demos. The file names tell you what they show: webgl_shadowmap, webgl_loader_gltf, ...
  • Local manual - the official manual, chapter by chapter.

A workflow that works well: find something in the examples that resembles what you want to make, copy it to a folder of your own, and take it apart until you understand which line does what. Taking apart is learning.

Where to next?

You have already seen shadows, models and shaders in this course. These are the logical next hills:

  1. Post-processing

    Effects over your whole image: bloom (glow), depth of field, grain. Look for the examples starting with webgl_postprocessing in your clone - the EffectComposer is the keyword.

  2. Physics

    Real gravity, collisions and bouncing connect to three.js through a physics library such as Rapier or Cannon. three.js draws, the physics engine calculates - you connect the positions every frame.

  3. WebXR

    The same scene in virtual or augmented reality. Because you already use setAnimationLoop, you are closer than you think: look for the webxr examples in your clone.

Did you know

three.js is building a WebGPU renderer, the successor to WebGL - the same move p5.js 2.x is making. The concepts from this manual (scene, camera, mesh, light) stay exactly the same; only the engine underneath gets faster.
